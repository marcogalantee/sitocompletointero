Push-Up

how to do it:

Start in a plank position with hands aligned under your shoulders, legs straight, and body in a straight line. Lower your chest until it nearly touches the floor, then push back up to return to the starting position.

What is it for:

Strengthens and stabilizes the upper body, increasing arm and shoulder endurance and improving body control during pushing movements.

Diamond Push-Up

how to do it:

Similar to a traditional push-up, but place your hands together under your chest, forming a diamond shape. Lower your chest toward your hands, then push up.

What is it for:

Increases arm strength, focusing on the inner arm area to improve stability in movements requiring precision.

Plyometric (Explosive) Push-Up

how to do it:

Perform a regular push-up, but push off forcefully to lift your hands off the floor, landing with control.

What is it for:

Enhances explosiveness and speed, preparing you for quick movements that require coordination and strength.

Chair Dips

how to do it:

Place two chairs beside your hips and rest your hands on them. Lower yourself until your elbows form a 90-degree angle, then push back up.

What is it for:

Increases endurance in downward pressing movements, improving your ability to support your body weight.

Pike Push-Up

how to do it:

Begin in a downward-facing dog position (as in yoga), lower your head toward the floor by bending your elbows, then push back up.

What is it for:

Strengthens the shoulders and prepares the body for vertical pressing movements, helpful for stabilizing the upper body in upward movements.

Plank

how to do it:

Lie face down and lift yourself onto your forearms and toes, keeping your body in a straight line.

What is it for:

Strengthens the core to improve trunk stability, essential for supporting the body in nearly all physical movements.

Side Plank

how to do it:

Lie on your side, supporting your body on one forearm and lifting your body off the floor.

What is it for:

Improves lateral core stability and helps maintain balance, critical for movements that require an upright posture.

Mountain Climbers

how to do it:

In a high plank position, bring one knee toward your chest, quickly alternating legs.

What is it for:

Increases cardiovascular endurance, prepares the core for dynamic movements, and strengthens leg agility.

Leg Raises

how to do it:

Lying on your back, lift your legs to a 90-degree angle, then lower them slowly.

What is it for:

Strengthens the lower core, important for stabilizing the back and improving posture.

Bicycle Crunches

how to do it:

Lying on your back, simulate a pedaling motion by bringing your elbow toward the opposite knee.

What is it for:

Improves coordination and strengthens twisting core movements, essential for actions that require torso rotation.

Squat

how to do it:

Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, bend your knees and lower as if sitting in an imaginary chair, then stand back up.

What is it for:

Improves leg stability and strength, essential for lifting movements and maintaining proper posture during walking and running.

Lunges

how to do it:

From a standing position, step forward with one leg, bending both knees to form 90-degree angles. Stand back up and repeat on the other side.

What is it for:

Increases endurance and balance, useful for walking and running movements, and improves the ability to balance the body on one leg.

Side Lunges

how to do it:

Step to the side and bend one knee while keeping the other leg straight, then return to the starting position.

What is it for:

Strengthens lateral movements, improving leg flexibility and agility, essential in sports requiring changes in direction.

Squat Jumps

how to do it:

Perform a squat and push up into a jump, landing in a squat position.

What is it for:

Increases leg explosiveness and power, useful for movements that require quick strength, such as jumping and sprinting.

Pistol Squat (Single-Leg Squat)

how to do it:

Lie face down and lift yourself onto your forearms and toes, keeping your body in a straight line.

What is it for:

Strengthens the core to improve trunk stability, essential for supporting the body in nearly all physical movements.

Burpees

how to do it:

From a standing position, squat down, do a push-up, then jump up with your arms extended.

What is it for:

Improves cardiovascular endurance and explosiveness, engaging the whole body to increase endurance.

High Knees

how to do it:

Run in place, lifting your knees as high as possible toward your chest.

What is it for:

Increases speed and endurance, developing leg control to enhance movement quickness.

Jumping Jacks

how to do it:

If you have a jump rope, jump continuously while maintaining rhythm; you can add variations like cross jumps or double jumps.

What is it for:

Increases agility and cardiovascular endurance, improving coordination between hands and feet.

Bear Crawl

how to do it:

Start on hands and feet with knees slightly off the ground and walk forward and backward.

What is it for:

Strengthens the core and improves coordination and functional strength, useful for movements requiring stability and trunk control.

Skipping Rope

how to do it:

If you have a jump rope, jump continuously while maintaining rhythm; you can add variations like cross jumps or double jumps.

What is it for:

Increases agility and cardiovascular endurance, improving coordination between hands and feet.
